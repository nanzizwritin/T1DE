About Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes is a condition where the body's immune system stops the pancreas from making insulin — the hormone that lets your body use glucose (sugar) from food for energy. Because of this, blood glucose has to be managed with insulin every day.

Understanding your glucose numbers

Blood glucose is usually measured in mg/dL. A common general target range is 70–180 mg/dL, though your own targets are set by your doctor.

  • Below 70 (hypoglycaemia / "hypo"): too low. Can cause shakiness, sweating, confusion. Usually treated quickly with fast-acting sugar.
  • 70–180 (in range): the general goal for most of the day.
  • Above 180–200 (hyperglycaemia / "hyper"): too high. Frequent highs over time can affect long-term health.

Why monitoring matters

Tracking glucose and insulin regularly helps you and your care team spot patterns and adjust care. Recording values consistently is one of the most useful things you can do between visits.

Insulin basics

Most people with T1D use two kinds of insulin: basal (long-acting, working in the background) and bolus (taken around meals). Keeping doses and timing consistent helps keep glucose steadier.

This information is general education about Type 1 diabetes, not medical advice. Always follow the guidance of your own doctor or diabetes care team.
